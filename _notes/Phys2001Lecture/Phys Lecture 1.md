# Types of Quantities in Physics
- Scalar: A quantity that has a numerical value (magnitude) but no direction.
- Vector: A quantity with both numerical value (magnitude) and direction.
	- Use + and - signs to indicate vector's directions
# Section 2.1: Position, Velocity, and Speed of a Particle
## Position X
- Location of the particle with respect to a chosen reference point
- If you know the position of a particle at all times, then you can describe its motion.
## Displacement $\Delta$ X
- The change in the position of a particle in some time interval
- As the particle moves from an initial position to a final position, its displacement is given by:
$$
\Delta x = x_f - x_i
$$
$x_f$ : final position
$x_i$ : initial position
SI Unit: meter (m)
Vector quantity

There is a difference between displacement and distance traveled.
- Distance: the length of a path followed by a particle - Scalar quantity
- Can have a zero displacement in a time interval but a nonzero distance.
## Average Velocity $v_{x,avg}$
- The particle's displacement divided by the time interval in which that displacement occurs
$$
v_{x,avg} = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}
$$
- Can be positive or negative
- SI unit: $\frac{meters}{second}$ ($\frac{m}{s}$)
- Vector quantity
- Again, be careful. Average velocity and average speed are not the same
### Average Speed $v_{avg}$
- Defined as the particle's total distance divided by the time interval
$$
v_{avg} = \frac{d}{\Delta t}
$$
- Always positive
- SI Unit: m/s
Scalar quantity
### Instantaneous Velocity $v_x$
- The velocity of a particle at a particular instant in time t
- Slope of the tangent line at a point on a position vs. time graph
$$
v_x = \lim_{\Delta t\to0} \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dx}{dt}
$$
- In calculus, this limit is called the derivative of x with respect to t.
- Instantaneous velocity can be positive, negative, or zero.
