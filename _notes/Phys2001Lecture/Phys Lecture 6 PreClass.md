We learned in class that any 2-d vector $\overrightarrow{A}$ can be constructed using the corresponding x and y coordinates, A $_{x}$ and A $_y$, and the unit vectors, $\hat{i}$ and $\hat{j}$. Considering that the vector components were obtained by multiplying A $_x$ (A $_y$) by the appropriate unit vector, $\hat{i}$ ($\hat{j}$), it seems it should be possible to find a unit vector $\hat{A}$ that points in the direction of $\hat{A}$ (rather than xor y-) so that it can be expressed as:
$$
\overrightarrow{A}=A \times \hat{A}
$$
a. Solve the above vector equation to get a vector equation for $\hat{A}$.
$$
\hat{A} \equiv \frac{\overrightarrow{A}}{A} = \frac{A_x\hat{i} + A_y\hat{j}}{\sqrt{A_x^2 + A_y^2}}
$$
b. A velocity, $\overrightarrow{v}$, is represented as (v $_x$, v $_y$) = (12.0 m/s, 16.0 m/s). Calculate the corresponding speed (the magnitude of the velocity).
$$
\sqrt{12^2+16^2}=20 \text{ m/s}
$$
c. Using the equation you found for a., express the unit vector that points in the same direction as $\overrightarrow{v}$, as a vector with x and y-component vectors.
$$
\frac{12}{20}\hat{i}+\frac{16}{20}\hat{j}= 0.6\hat{i}+0.8\hat{j}
$$
