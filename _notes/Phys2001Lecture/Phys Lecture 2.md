## Instantaneous Speed
- The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity
- No direction associated with it
- Your car's speedometer measures the instantaneous speed

Example:
The position of a car on a road is given by
$$
x(t) = 16m - (10.1 \frac{m}{s})t + (1.10 \frac{m}{s^2})t^2
$$
a) What is the car's velocity as a function of time?
$$
v(t) = -10.1\frac{m}{s} + (2.2\frac{m}{s^2})t
$$
b) What is the car's velocity at 1 second?
$$-7.9 \frac{m}{s}$$
## First Analysis Model: Particle under Constant Velocity
- Used in any situation in which the object can be modeled as a particle moving with constant velocity
- Constant velocity means the instantaneous velocity during a time interval is the same as the average velocity over the interval
$$
v_x = v_{\text{x,avg}}
$$
$$
v_x = \frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}
	$$
And since $\Delta x = x_f - x_i$
$x_f = x_i + v_x t$
(for constant $v_x$ where $t_i = 0$)
## Another Analysis Model: Particle under Constant Speed
$$
v = \frac{d}{\Delta t}
$$
where d is the distance traveled
# Acceleration
- The change in velocity divided by the time interval during which that change occurs:
$$
		a_{\text{x,avg}} = \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_{xf} - v_{xi}}{t_f - t_i}
$$
- SI unit: meters/second ^2 = m/s^2
Vector quantity
## Instantaneous Acceleration
$$
a_x = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta v_x}{\Delta t} = \frac{dv_x}{dt}
$$
### Constant Velocity = Zero Acceleration
Acceleration doesn't have to be in the same direction as motion.
# Constant Acceleration (kinematic equations)
- The velocity changes at the same rate throughout the motion
$$
a_x = \frac{v_{xf} - v_{xi}}{t_f-0}
$$
- Can determine an object's velocity at any time if we knwo the object's initial velocity
