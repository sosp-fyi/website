Pressure = force distributed over area
$$
P = \frac{F}{A}
$$
SI unit: N/m $^2$ = pascal (Pa), atm = 1.01 · 10 $^5$ Pa

P is a scalar (like string tension) in a fluid

- Vector force on a surface from fluid pressure
	- on a ~flat surface, area A: magnitude F = PA
	- Direction: normal (perpendicular) to area
	- P exists throughout a fluid, may have different values at different locations

# Macroscopic Description of an Ideal Gas
Unlike solids & liquids, gases
- Are compressible - volume (v) ↔ pressure (P) (temperature, …)
- Fill the volume of the container; Pressure ~ constant over volume

### The Equation of State
the relationship between volume (V), pressure (P), temperature (T, in k)
For "ideal gas":
- At constant temperature, pressure is inversely proportional to its volume (Boyle's law)
$$
P \propto 1/V
$$
- At constant pressure, volume is directly proportional to absolute temperature (Charles' law)
$$
V \propto T
$$
- At constant volume, pressure is directly proportional to temperature (Gay-Lussac's law)
$$
P \propto T
$$
- And P, V are proportional to the number of gas molecules, N

combined to get Equation of state for ideal gas
$$
PV = Nk_B T
$$
Boltzmann's constant: $k_B = 1.38 * 10^{-23}$ J/K

- or for # of molecules in moles (n)
$$
PV = nRT
$$
Universal gas constant: R = 8.314 J/mol * K = 0.08214 L * atm/mol * K

1 mole = Avogadro's number $6.022*10^{23}$
Mass of 1 mole of hydrogen atoms = 1 g (10 $^{-3}$ kg)
Many real gasses at low pressure behave as ideal gases

$$
n = \frac{m \text{( g)}}{\text{mass num (g/mol)}}
$$
