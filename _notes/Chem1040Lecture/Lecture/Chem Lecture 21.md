## Bookkeeping: Quantum Numbers and Atomic Orbitals - Finding an Electron's “home address”
An atomic orbital is specified by three quantum numbers.

Terminology:
1. n - gives level or shell — size and energy of the orbital (i.e., how far electron is from the nucleus) — higher number = larger orbital and higher energy
2. l - gives sublevel or subshell — describes what shape the orbital home is
	1. l = 0 is s, l = 1 is p, l = 2 is d, l = 3 is f, then g…
	2. Notation is: nl → 2p, 3d, etc.
3. m $_l$ - exact orientation of orbital home

2p is a figure 8
3d is a clover shape
4f has 8 balloon shapes going out into each quadrant on the 3d plane

Example:
3p is n = 3, l = 1, m $_l$ = 1

# Different Ways to Represent Electrons
## Electron Configuration/Building Orbital Diagrams
First the aufbau principle is applied - electrons are always placed in the lowest energy sublevel available.
H(Z=1)1s $^1$
1s = ↑

The exclusion principle states that each orbital may contain a maximum of 2 electrons, which must have opposite spins.
He(Z=2)1s $^2$  
1 s = ↓ and ↑

## Building Orbital Diagrams
Hund's rule specifies that when orbitals of equal energy are available (the same n and l values), the lowest energy electron configuration has the maximum number of unpaired electrons with parallel spins.
