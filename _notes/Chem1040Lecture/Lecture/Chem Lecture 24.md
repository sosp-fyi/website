# Acid-Base Behaviors of Oxides
Main-group metals - Ionic oxides - basic in aqueous solution.
Main-group nonmetals - covalent oxides - acidic in aqueous solution.

Some metals and metalloids - amphoteric oxides - act as acids or bases in water. 
# Electron Configurations of Monatomic Ions
Elements at either end of a period gain or lose electrons to attain a filled outer lever. The resulting ion will have a noble gas electron configuration and is said to be isoelectronic with that noble gas.

Hint: draw the electron configuration for the neutral atom first, and then add/subtract electrons as needed to make the ion.

A pseudo-noble gas configuration is attained when a metal atom empties its highest energy level.
The ion attains the stability of empty ns and np sublevels and a filled (n-1)d sublevel.
Sn([Kr] 5s$^2$ 4d$^{10}$ 5p$^2$) → 4e$^-$ + Sn$^{4+}$ ([Kr] 4d$^{10}$)

A metal may lose only the np electrons to attain an inert pair configuration.
The ion attains the stability of a filled ns and (n - 1)d sublevels.
Sn([Kr] 5s$^2$ 4d$^{10}$ 5p^2) → 2e$^-$ + Sn$^{2+}$ ([Kr] 5s$^2$ 4d$^{10}$)

Basically, ions will form by getting rid of the electrons that are furthest away from the nucleus first.

# Magnetic Properties of Transition Metal Ions
A species with unpaired electron(s) is paramagnetic - it is attracted by a magnetic field.
Example: Ag

A species with all electrons paired is diamagnetic - it is slightly repelled by a magnetic field.
Example: Cd
# Ionic vs. Atomic Radii
For ions/atoms with the same number of electrons, size decreases as nuclear charge (Z) increases.

Ion size trend is the same for ions with the same nearest noble gas.
