A system that does no work on the surroundings (and vise versa), but which transfers heat to the surroundings, has q < 0, $\Delta$ E < 0

q is heat
w is work
$\Delta$ E is change in energy

A system transfers 225 J of heat to surroundings and does 645 J of work on surroundings. $\Delta$ E = -870 J

# Law of Energy Conservation
First law of thermodynamics: the total energy of the universe is constant.
Energy is neither created nor destroyed, but transferred as heat and/or work.
$\Delta$ E $_{universe}$ = $\Delta$ E $_{system}$ + $\Delta$ E $_{surroundings}$ = 0

# Units of Energy
The SI unit of energy is the Joule (J).
1 J = 1 kg*m $^2$ /s $^2$
calorie - once defined in terms of water - (warm up 1 g of water by 1 $\degree$ C)
1 cal = 4.184 J
British Thermal Unit (Btu)
(warm up 1 lb of water by 1 $\degree$ F)
1 Btu is equivalent to 1055 J.

State function: one whose value depends ONLY on current state of system - not on path to get to current state.
E is a state function.
Therefore, $\Delta$ E is independent of process path.

w = -P $\Delta$ V
An expanding gas pushing back the atmosphere does PV work (w = -P $\Delta$ V).

Energy change is: $\Delta$ E = q + w
At constant pressure: $\Delta$ E = q $_p$ - P $\Delta$ V
q $_p$ = $\Delta$ E + P $\Delta$ V

Since PV work can be hard to measure, we introduce a new state function called enthalpy (H):
H = E + PV
At constant pressure: $\Delta$ H = $\Delta$ E + P $\Delta$ V
From above: q $_p$ = $\Delta$ E + P $\Delta$ V
Therefore: $\Delta$ H = q $_p$
change in enthalpy = heat absorbed or released at constant pressure

# $\Delta$ H as a Measure of $\Delta$ E
$\Delta$ H ≈ $\Delta$ E
- for reactions that do not involve gases
- for reactions in which the total amount (mol) of gas does not change
- for reactions in which q $_p$ is much larger than P $\Delta$ V, even if the total mol of gas does change.

In which of these reactions is $\Delta$ H significantly different from $\Delta$ E
CO $_2$ (s) → CO $_2$ (g)

## Enthalpy Diagrams for Exothermic and Endothermic Processes
Exothermic process - heat is released. $\Delta$ H is negative
Endothermic process - heat is absorbed. $\Delta$ H is positive

H $_2$ (g) → 2H (g) is an endothermic process

$\Delta$ E = q + w
At constant volume, w = 0, so
q $_V$ = $\Delta$ E
At constant pressure, define new state function, so that
q $_P$ = $\Delta$ H
