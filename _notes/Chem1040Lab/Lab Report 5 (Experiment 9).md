# Introduction

The main objectives for this experiment were to determine temperature changes in a set of chemical processes, determine enthalpy changes for the same chemical processes, compare enthalpy changes to literature values, and to test the validity of Hess's Law. Determining the temperature changes in chemical processes was accomplished by taking the initial and final temperature from the reaction and finding the difference. The enthalpy change was determined by using the standard enthalpy of reaction formula and inputting the values from the experiment. Comparing the enthalpy changes to literary values was achieved by taking the equated enthalpy changes and comparing that to the literary values. Testing the validity of Hess's law was done by finding the difference in enthalpy change between the solid first and second pathway.

The concepts that relate lab and lecture are change in enthalpy, Hess's Law, and exothermic/endothermic reactions.

The lab techniques used were precise measurements with glassware and the analytical balance, measuring temperature using the temperature probe, and graphing the temperature of the mixture over time.

# Conclusion

The objective of determining the temperature change during the chemical reactions was achieved by using the temperature probe to check the change in temperature before and after the liquid and solid were added to the original solution. The changes in enthalpy for those chemical processes were determined by taking the initial temperatures and mass of the reactants and then taking the final temperatures and applying the standard enthalpy of reaction formula to find the enthalpy change. These enthalpy changes were then compared to the literary values found from chem21labs and using the enthalpy change of products minus the enthalpy change of reactants to find the overall enthalpy change. The validity of Hess's Law was tested by finding the difference between the two overall enthalpy changes values for the first and second reaction. If the value was 0 (or close when accounting for experimental error), then Hess's Law was obeyed. Our experiment proved that Hess's Law was obeyed for both reactions because our difference between overall enthalpy changes was ~20 which is close enough to 0 when accounting for experimental error.
