# Introduction

The objective of the fourth experiment was to determine the identity of ions from a series of unknowns. This was accomplished by taking the initial unknown substances and performing various tests on them. These tests include: litmus test, burn test, and mixing the solutions together. After observing the results from the tests certain aspects of each solution could be determined.

The concepts that relate lab and lecture are acid-base reactions, balancing chemical equations, and precipitate reactions.

The lab techniques used were litmus testing, fire safety when performing the burn test, and general lab safety when dealing with hazardous chemicals.

# Explanation for Results

Unknown number 3332-75

The whole process starts with E. It turned blue to red in the litmus test, indicating that it was acidic, which means it contained H3O. The flame test produced no color, meaning no metal cation was present. When E was mixed with other unknowns it formed a white precipitate with G indicating AgCl was present. This means that E was H3O and Cl, which put together makes HCl.

F is Na (given from the flame test) and is a base (litmus test). During the mixing test bubbles showed up when F was combined with acidic E which means there was CO3 reacting with H3O to release a gas. Also, F formed cloudy precipitates with metal cation solutions such as A (Co). These things mean that F is sodium carbonate Na2CO3. 

Unknown D also made a red flame, which means that it is either Li or Sr, but its litmus test (red to blue) means that it is basic. When D was combined with G a brown precipitate formed this is likely from the inclusion of Ag in the reaction. Also, D didn't produce bubbles when mixed with acid E meaning it doesn't include CO3. This means that D is LiOH.

Unknown G didn't show any flame and was neutral on the litmus test. This likely means that it is a neutral salt. It can be deduced that D includes Ag because of the results from E. This also means that it is either NO3 or SO4. When mixed with other neutral unknowns and nitrate, it formed no precipitate. This means that it has NO3 and becomes AgNO3.

Unknown C produced a green flame, which means that it's cation is Ba. The litmus results show that it is neutral. When C was mixed with G (AgNO3) a white precipitate formed meaning that there are chloride ions in the mixture. This means that C includes Cl. So C is BaCl2.

Unknown B had a red flame like D, but the litmus test proved it was neutral. When mixed with A, it became a cloudy white. Due to the flame test it was already between Li and Sr and the mixture being a cloudy white proved that it's cation is Sr. When mixed with other cations B didn't produce anything meaning it is a soluble nitrate. So, B is Sr(NO3)2.

Lastly, unknown A. It produced no flame color and showed no change during the litmus test meaning it is a neutral salt. When mixed with D or F a precipitate formed due to the cobalt in A. Also, at this point there is only one cation left, so Co was determined to be in A. Nevertheless, a cloudy mixture was formed when A was mixed with B (Sr) meaning A likely had SO4 because SrSO4 is insoluble. This means that A is CoSO4.
