# Introduction

The objectives of Experiment 5 was to standardize a NaOH solution by titrating it against a solution of potassium hydrogen phthalate and to determine the equivalent mass of an unknown acid by titrating it with the standardized NaOH solution. The first objective was accomplished by finding the point where the acid turned a light pink due to mixing NaOH into it. After finding this point the same procedure was followed for 4 trials to find an average molarity by finding the moles and dividing by the volume. The second objective was achieved by weighing the unknown acid, titrating until the light pink appeared and then finding the equivalents by finding the moles of it and taking the grams divided by the equivalents. An average for that was also found by repeating the procedure 4 times. 

The concepts that relate lab to lecture are molarity, acid-base reactions, and equivalence points.

The lab techniques that were used were accurate measurements using the analytical balance, using a buret, and performing a titration.

# Questions

1. How would your calculated molarity of the NaOH solution be affected (too high, too low, no change) by each of the following errors? Explain each answer.
    1. The buret is rinsed with water, but not NaOH solution, before it is filled.
        1. Too low, The NaOH would be diluted from the water, so the molarity would be lower than expected. The volume increased, but the moles stayed the same, so moles/volume will result in a lower molarity than normal.
    2. Some KHC8H4O4 is spilled out of the flask after it is weighed.
        1. Too high, The mass recorded would be greater than the amount used in the titration, so there would be fewer moles, so when doing moles/volume it would be higher than expected.
    3. Some solution splashes out of the flask during titration.
        1. Too high, It would be too high because some acid in the flask would splash out, and the NaOH would need to react with less than expected to reach the titration point.
    4. You go past the end point in the titration.
        1. Too low, It would be too low because the amount of moles stays the same, but the volume would be greater than expected. So when finding the molarity it would be lower than it should be.

2. How would your calculated value for the equivalent mass of the acid be affected (too high, too low, no change) by each of the following errors? Explain each answer.
    1. Some acid is spilled out of the flask after it is weighed.
        1. The mass of the unknown would stay the same in the numerator, but the denominator would be lower than expected meaning that it would be too high.
    2. Some solution splashes out of the flask during titration.
        1. The mass of unknown stays the same (numerator) while the volume of NaOH used would decrease because some acid spilled out. So that would mean that the numerator stays the same while the denominator decreases meaning that it would be higher than expected.
    3. You go past the end point in the titration.
        1. The volume would increase meaning that the moles would be greater than expected while the mass stayed the same, so it would be lower than expected for the equivalence mass.
