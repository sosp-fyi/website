# Introduction

The main objective for the second experiment was to determine the molar ratio in which two compounds react for several reactions, using thermometric titration. This was accomplished by measuring the temperature change during a reaction between two reactants in varying ratios. The temperature was graphed on a temperature vs. time graph where the stoichiometric point could be determined. The concepts that relate the lab to the lecture are stoichiometry, molarity, balanced equations, and chemical reactions. The lab techniques used were measured volumes, measuring temperature using the temperature probe, and using the drop counter to convert drops of solution added into volume of solution added.

## Experimental Procedure

The procedure followed was what was provided in the handout downloaded from Chem21labs.com Experiment 2: Reaction Stoichiometry By Thermometric Titration pg. 3-5.

### **1.** Why Does the Temperature of the Reaction Mixture Drop (as Opposed to Remaining constant) once the Reaction Reaches the Stoichiometric Point?

This is due to the limiting reactant being fully used at the stoichiometric point. So there is no longer anything for the excess to react with, hence the temperature of the mixture drops.

### **2.** For One of the Reactions, a Colored Product Temporarily Appeared. For Which Reaction Did This Occur? What Do You Think This Product Was?

A colored product temporarily appeared in the reaction between NaOCl and KI. This product was likely iodine and was formed because the iodide from the KI is converted into I_2 which forms a yellowish color in water.

# Conclusion

The objective of determining the molar ratio from the reactions between two compounds was achieved by adding one solution to the other until there was no longer an increase in temperature. The number of moles of each reactant was then calculated at this point where its mole ratio was determined. When this ratio is rounded to a whole number gave the molar ratios shown above. The fact that the calculated mole ratios were different from what was expected can be attributed to experimental error and the high probability that one of the reactants was contaminated during the experiment.
