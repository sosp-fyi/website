# Discussion

1. In the spectrophotometric method, you were instructed to obtain the phosphate stock solution in a dry beaker. Why is this? How would the slope of your calibration curve be affected if your beaker was not dry?
   If the beaker is not dry, any leftover water in the beaker would dilute the phosphate stock solution before the experiment even started. This would mean that the actual concentration would be lower than the true stock concentration. And when preparing the calibration standards, their true phosphate concentrations would be lower than the values calculated.
   The effect on the slope would be that the slope of the calibration curve is smaller than it should be. This is because all the solutions are lower concentration then their absorbance will also be lower.
2. In the gravimetric method, is it important that you dissolve your sample in exactly 10 mL of water? Why or why not?
   No, the exact initial volume of water does not matter. The gravimetric analysis depends on the amount of phosphate present in the sample, forming an insoluble solid with all the phosphate, and filtering, drying, and weighing that solid. As long as the sample fully dissolves and none of the solution is lost, using different amounts of water won't change the outcome, the same amount of phosphate will precipitate and be weighed.
3. In the protocol considerations, it was suggested that you dissolve the fertilizer, then filter it before performing either the gravimetric or spectrophotometric procedure to remove any insoluble materials.
    1. How would your calculated %P₂O₅ value be affected if you had not filtered? Explain your answer.
       If insoluble particles stay in the sample, they do not contain phosphate that reacts in either procedure, but their mass is included in the amount of fertilizer weighed. This means the mass of fertilizer is too large, but the amount of phosphate measured stays the same. So the calculated %P₂O₅ will appear too low, because the correct amount of phosphorus is being divided by a mass that includes non-reactive materials.
    2. What experiment could you perform to see if the presence of insoluble materials really does have a significant effect on your calculated %P₂O₅ value?
       A simple experiment that could be performed to test whether insoluble materials would affect the calculated %P₂O₅ would be: Take one fertilizer sample and divide it into two equal parts. Filter one sample, and leave the other unfiltered. Perform the full procedure on each. Then compare the calculated %P₂O₅ values. If the unfiltered sample consistently gives lower values, the insoluble materials are affecting the measurement.
4. In the protocol considerations, it was suggested that you use a blank solution containing the fertilizer solution when obtaining the absorbance spectra in the spectrophotometric procedure to account for the presence of light absorbing dyes in the fertilizer.
    1. How would your calculated %P₂O₅ value be affected if you had not used this blank solution? Explain your answer.
       Some fertilizers contain dyes that absorb light when performing a spectrophotometric calculation. If a blank isn't used, the spectrophotometer thinks the absorbance is coming from the phosphate, when part of it is really coming from the dye. This makes the absorbance appear larger than it really is for the given concentration of phosphate. This will lead to a calculated concentration of phosphate that is too high, which means that the calculated %P₂O₅ will be too high.
    2. What experiment could you perform to see if the presence of dyes in the fertilizer really does have a significant effect on your calculated %P₂O₅ value?
       An experiment that could be performed to test the effect of dyes would be to dissolve some fertilizer to make the sample solution. Then prepare two sets of colored solutions. For one set, use the proper fertilizer blank (fertilizer and water but no blue complex) and the other could be a regular reagent blank (water and reagents). Then compare the absorbances and calculated %P₂O₅. If the values differ significantly, the dye has noticeable effects on the results.
5. In the gravimetric method, why is the reaction solution tested with litmus paper?
   The litmus paper is used to test if enough ammonia has been added. If the paper stays red after adding some ammonia, it means more needs to be added. If the paper turns blue, it means that enough ammonia has been added, and you can continue on with the procedure. If the paper doesn't turn blue, but the procedure continues, then the precipitate won't form completely, so there will be less solid than expected weighed out and the %P₂O₅ will come out too low. The litmus paper is a way to check that the proceeding reaction will occur as planned.

# Conclusion

The purpose of this experiment was to compare two methods for determining the %P₂O₅ in fertilizer and recommend the more reliable one. The gravimetric method gave a value of 28.0%, which is reasonably close to the expected 30%, indicating good accuracy. Small losses of precipitate or incomplete drying may have caused the slight deviation.

The spectrophotometric method gave a much higher value of 47.9%, which is not a reasonable number for the fertilizer. This was likely caused by an experimental error. Our best guess for why this number is so high is because we didn't filter the fertilizer before starting the procedure (this wasn't laid out for us in the given procedure).

Based on our experimental trials, the gravimetric method was much superior compared to the spectrophotometric method. The gravimetric method was also much easier to complete experimentally. It required significantly less time, much less lab equipment, and was much easier to complete the calculations for. The two dilutions performed in the spectrophotometric method were quite confusing, as well as the necessity to create a graph to get the slope of the calibration curve. Overall, the gravimetric method seems like the better choice because it gave more accurate results, was easier to perform, and the final calculations were much easier to compute.
