# Introduction

The objective for experiment 3 was the synthesis of an alum, the separation and purification of the product, and to assess the purity of the product by determining the solid-to-liquid temperature range. The first objective was accomplished by dissolving aluminum foil in KOH, and subsequently adding H2SO4 to the reaction mixture. This addition formed a precipitate in the beginning, but after continued addition the precipitate dissolved and the alum was formed. The second object was achieved by placing the solution in ice cold water to form a precipitate. Then a vacuum funnel was set up to purify the solution by washing it with ice cold water. After washing the solution until it was no longer acidic it was then dried by adding 20 mL of isopropyl alcohol and waiting until completely dry. The final objective was accomplished by analyzing the melting point of the product. The first temperature recorded was when liquid first appears and the second was when the solid was completely gone.

The concepts relate the lab to lecture are Acid-Base reactions, reactions that form precipitates, stoichiometry. 

The lab techniques used were usage of the temperature probe, safely handling chemicals, and creating a vacuum using a Büchner funnel to purify the product.

# Questions

1. Of the reactions described in Equation 3-1 through Equation 3-4, which represent acid-base reactions?
    - The equations 3-2 and 3-3 are acid-base reactions because they have an H3O+ that donates a proton to the hydroxide which neutralizes the base.

2. Why do you use a graduated cylinder to measure out the desired volume of KOH and H2SO4, rather than a pipet or a buret?
    - The use of the graduated cylinder instead of a pipet or buret was used because there is less chance of contamination. With a graduated cylinder the solution is poured directly into it whereas, filling a pipet requires it to be placed inside the solution, and if it isn't clean then it will contaminate it.

3. Given the way this experiment is carried out, it is impossible to recover 100% of the product that forms, no matter how carefully you work. Explain why. (Hint--consider a particular physical property of the product.)
    - It is impossible to recover 100% of the product because during the washing process the alum is rinsed with water. However, the alum is slightly soluble in water. The loss of the product was minimized by using ice cold water, but some still dissolved in the water. There could have also been product lost during transfers or if some was left on the filter paper or glassware.

4. Consider the aluminum foil that you used. What evidence do you have that the aluminum foil contained impurities? How would this affect your calculated percent yield of alum?
    - The evidence that the aluminum foil contained impurities is that our percent yield wasn't 100%. While this could be due to experimental factors, it is highly likely that it is due to external factors as well. Because the aluminum foil used was store-bought aluminum, rather than pure aluminum, it likely contained impurities that led to the percent yield not being close to 100%. This is exasperated due to the theoretical yield calculations assuming that pure aluminum foil will be used.

5. In step 8 of the procedure, you washed the product with ice-cold water. If this were not done, what ions, which were used in excess of the stoichiometric amounts, would contaminate the product? Hint: think of all the ions that are in excess.
    - If the product were not washed with ice-cold water, the alum would be contaminated by excess K $​^+$ ​, OH $​^-$ ​, H $_3$ ​O $^​+$ ​, and SO​ $_4$ $^​2-$ ​ ions from the KOH and H $_2$ ​SO $​_4$ ​ solutions. By washing the alum these soluble impurities are removed while minimizing the loss of the alum because it is only slightly soluble in ice-cold water.

6. In many syntheses, yield must be sacrificed for purity. For this synthesis, what particular step of the procedure increased the purity of the product, but decreased the yield of the product?
    - The step that increased purity while reducing the yield was the washing step. This reduced the excess ions from the alum, but decreased the yield because some of the alum was also dissolved in the water.

# Conclusion

The objective of synthesizing an alum was achieved through a series of acid-base and precipitation reactions. The product was purified by crystalizing it and then washing it with ice-cold water, this improved the purity, but reduced the yield due to its slight solubility in water. The percent yield reflected these experimental loses and because of impurities in the aluminum. The purity of the product was then determined by analyzing its solid-to-liquid transition temperature range. Our narrow temperature range indicated that the alum was relatively pure.
