For the most part, I can instantly recognize a problem with someone's argument. However, there are times when I'm not really paying attention or someone is a really persuasive speaker, and it can be a little harder to recognize a flaw in their reasoning. For example, I sometimes listen to a podcast about a few guys just talking about random things and events happening in their lives. Sometimes when they are arguing about something unserious or inconsequential, I will pay little attention to the fortitude of the argument. However, if I'm in class and I hear a fault in someone's reasoning, then I will almost immediately pick up on it and recognize the error in their thought process. I then think of a way they could have worded it to be a stronger argument.  
  
In real life I see a lot of the Texas Sharpshooter fallacy, where someone will cherry-pick data so it will suit their argument without looking at or acknowledging any information that might disprove their point. It's widespread to see this with politics. For example, if a gun control and safety bill is passed, it is likely that they will highlight lower crime rates. But they won't take into effect the other events happening in the world that could have also caused lower crime rates (seen from the bipartisan Safer Communities Act). The outcome from this situation is usually in favor of the politician because they passed a law that reduced violent crime. When in reality, it was largely due to external factors that reduced crime.

I think it is incredibly important for college students to recognize the sharpshooter fallacy because it is one of the most common ways to mislead people. One of the most important things when writing a good research paper is having convincing and useful data. While cherry-picking specific data to confirm a bias is easy, it creates an easy flaw to exploit in the paper. By choosing data that conveys the complete picture, the argument that is being claimed will be stronger. I think the most important thing to make sure of when choosing data is looking at multiple sources. By confirming that the data is consistent across multiple articles, it will be clear that the information is not falling under the sharpshooter fallacy.
