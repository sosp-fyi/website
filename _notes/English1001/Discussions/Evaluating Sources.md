# The Ethical Use of Consumer Data in Targeted Advertising

## Popular Source
[Tufts Now. (2023, September 7). The Argument for Digital Privacy.](https://now.tufts.edu/2023/09/07/argument-digital-privacy)
### Evaluation
#### Rhetorical Appeals
- Ethos: The author established credibility through their connections to university faculty. The website with which this article was published through also leads to some credibility because it is a university website. By leaning into these, the author comes across as credible and knowledgeable about the subject.
- Pathos: The piece appeals to readers' sense of vulnerability by describing how things like metadata and behavioral data can expose intimate and personal details about their lives. By framing privacy as a human right under threat, it evokes concern and fear for the general audience emotionally.
- Logos: The article supports its arguments with logical reasoning. It explains how current laws are outdated compared to modern technology. It provides examples, such as how smartphone data and app permissions reveal personal patterns, to connect daily behavior to potential privacy risks.  
#### CRAAP Method
- Currency: Published in 2023, it's somewhat current, but still extremely relevant considering these concerns are still  
  a high priority.
- Relevance: The article directly relates to the ethical use of consumer data, making it extremely relevant when talking about targeted ads and the ethical use of data.
- Authority: The author, Mike Silver, isn't a direct expert in the field. He is the associate director of public relations at Tufts University. He has a Ph.D. in biochemistry and molecular biology from Harvard and a B.A. from Columbia University, so his degrees aren't related to the technology field.
- Accuracy: The information is clear and aligns with other well-documented publications about privacy concerns and data collection/usage. It lacks primary data but maintains accuracy due to expert interviews and reliable examples.
- Purpose/Point of View: The articles' purpose is to inform and persuade the audience to take their privacy seriously. It is more concerned about the ethical issues than the technical side, and it also shows a slight bias toward stronger privacy laws and protections.  
## Primary Scholarly Source
Hemker, Sophie, et al. “The Transformation of Data Marketing: How an Ethical Lens on Consumer Data Collection Shapes the Future of Marketing.” _Sustainability_, vol. 13, no. 20, 2021, p. 11208, https://doi.org/10.3390/su132011208.
### Evaluation
#### Rhetorical Appeals
- **Ethos:** The authors, Sven Hemker, Cristina Herrando, and Efthymios Constantinides, are affiliated with respected European universities such as the University of Twente and the University of Zaragoza. Their credibility is strengthened by the paper’s publication in _Sustainability_, a peer-reviewed, open-access journal published by MDPI that is recognized in the field of business ethics and sustainable marketing. The authors’ backgrounds in digital marketing and consumer ethics further enhance the trustworthiness of their claims.
- **Pathos:** Although the article maintains an academic tone, it elicits concern and reflection from readers by highlighting how invasive data collection and tracking practices can harm consumer trust and autonomy. The discussion of privacy violations and the potential misuse of consumer data creates an emotional appeal to ethical responsibility within the marketing industry.
- **Logos:** The paper is heavily grounded in logical reasoning and empirical synthesis. The authors employ a systematic literature review of over 100 peer-reviewed sources to identify ethical challenges in data-driven marketing. They use clear conceptual frameworks and categorize findings around transparency, consent, and fairness—providing a rational and structured argument for why marketing ethics must evolve alongside data technologies.
#### CRAAP Method
- **Currency:** Published in 2021, this article is current and reflects recent developments in data-driven marketing and privacy regulation (e.g., GDPR and CCPA). The authors discuss ongoing transformations in marketing ethics that remain highly relevant today as AI and personalization tools continue to advance.
- **Relevance:** This source is highly relevant to the topic of _The Ethical Use of Consumer Data in Targeted Advertising_. It directly examines how marketers collect, process, and deploy consumer data, and how ethical frameworks can shape responsible advertising practices. The focus on consumer privacy, consent, and transparency ties directly to your research question.
- **Authority:** All authors are academic experts in marketing, ethics, and digital transformation. Their institutional affiliations and the peer-reviewed nature of _Sustainability_ establish strong authority. Constantinides, in particular, is well known for his work on digital marketing strategy and ethical digital transformation, lending the paper significant scholarly weight.
- **Accuracy:** The article is methodologically sound, using systematic literature review methods and citing over a hundred peer-reviewed works. It presents findings objectively and aligns with recognized theories in marketing ethics, such as stakeholder theory and deontological ethics. Its conclusions are well-supported and transparent about limitations.
- **Purpose/Point of View:** The authors aim to inform and encourage ethical transformation within the marketing discipline. Their perspective advocates for responsible and transparent use of consumer data, emphasizing that ethical conduct is not only morally right but also essential for long-term consumer trust and sustainability. The tone remains academic and balanced rather than persuasive or sensational.
## Secondary Scholarly Source
Ali, Muhammad, et al. “Discrimination through Optimization: How Facebook’s Ad Delivery Can Lead to Biased Outcomes.” _Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction_, vol. 3, no. CSCW, 199, 2019, https://doi.org/10.1145/3359301.
### Evaluation
#### Rhetorical Appeals
- Ethos: The authors are affiliated with respected institutions such as Northeastern University, and the University of Southern California. Their credibility is reinforced by peer review and the paper's publication in the *Proceedings of the ACM on Human-Computer Interaction*, a respected academic journal.  
- Pathos: While the paper is written objectively, it still creates emotional concerns by revealing how Facebook's ad system unintentionally produces racial and gender bias.
- Logos: The authors rely entirely on experimentation and statistical reasoning to come to their conclusions. They present data showing how ad content, budget, and algorithmic optimization can lead to bias in ad delivery, even if unintentional.  
#### CRAAP Method
- Currency: Published in 2019, the source is somewhat up to date. The algorithms described in this study are still seen on Facebook now, so the information is still useful.
- Relevance: This source is very relevant to a topic on the ethical use of consumer data in targeted advertising. It  
  directly talks about how Facebook uses algorithmic targeting and optimization methods (based on user data) and how these can result in biased ad placement even if the advertisers don't intend for that to be the case.
- Authority: The authors are academic researchers and experts with backgrounds in computer science, human-computer interaction, and technology policy. Their affiliations with top universities and digital rights organizations make them highly credible and a good authority in this field.
- Accuracy: The information is supported by quantitative experiments, peer review, and public data. The authors disclose limitations and their methods clearly. Their findings are consistent with prior research on algorithmic discrimination, which indicates high accuracy.
- Purpose/Point of View: The purpose is informative and investigative. They were trying to prove that algorithmic processes can lead to discrimination, and they chose Facebook as the target to show this. The authors' point of view is for greater transparency and regulatory oversight on digital advertising. However, this is all shown from an objective point of view based on the data and evidence collected from their research. The topic that will be shown through the sources is The ethical use of consumer data in targeted advertising.
